Chapter 1: What You Will Learn

Introduction

Let me tell you about a booking that never happened.

It was late 2024, and I was planning a trip through Southeast Asia. Rather than spend hours clicking through tour operator websites myself, I asked an AI assistant to compare options and recommend the best itinerary.

The assistant analysed two operators and confidently recommended Company A over Company B. The reasoning seemed sound: Company A offered better value, more cultural experiences, and more comprehensive coverage of the region I wanted to visit.

I was about to book. Then I decided to check the recommendation myself.

Company B's website displayed their complete 14-day itinerary on a single page. You could scroll through the entire trip — temple visits in Thailand, market tours in Vietnam, ancient ruins in Cambodia, coastal relaxation in Malaysia. Everything was visible at once.

Company A's website used pagination. Day 1 on the first page, with a "Next" button to see Day 2. The AI assistant had looked at the first page, seen Day 1, and concluded that was the entire trip. It compared a single-day tour with a fourteen-day journey and recommended the wrong company.

I caught the error. But how many people wouldn't?

What This Book Covers

This book is about the collision between how we design websites and how AI agents use them. It's not speculation about a distant future — this is happening right now, affecting real businesses and real users.

AI agents visit websites to research products, compare options, check availability, book services, and complete transactions. ChatGPT browses e-commerce sites. Claude reads documentation. Autonomous shopping assistants attempt checkouts. Task automation tools fill out forms.

Many of them fail. Not loudly, not with error messages. Just silently, giving wrong information or reporting success when they've actually failed.

The patterns that break agents also break humans. Toast notifications that vanish before screen readers can announce them. Pagination that hides content from search engines. Single-page applications where state changes are invisible. Progressive disclosure that forces dozens of clicks to see full pricing. Form validation that only works after submission.

We've known about these problems for years. Accessibility advocates have been explaining them. But there wasn't enough commercial pressure to fix them.

Now there is.

When agents make purchasing decisions worth millions, businesses pay attention. When conversion funnels have invisible drop-offs, there's budget to fix them. When competitors' sites work for agents and yours doesn't, the commercial incentive becomes obvious.

This book explores the technical, business, ethical, and human implications of this shift...
